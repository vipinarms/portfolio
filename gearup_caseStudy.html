The Challenge

GEAR UP NC (Gaining Early Awareness and Readiness for Undergraduate Programs) is a federally funded initiative serving over 25,000 students across North Carolina‚Äîpredominantly first-generation college students from low-income families who have never set foot on a university campus.

The core challenge: How do you inspire college aspirations in students who can't physically visit campuses due to geographic, financial, or scheduling barriers?

Critical Barriers to College Access

Geographic Isolation

Many rural North Carolina students live 2-4 hours away from the nearest UNC campus. School districts lack funding for campus visit field trips, leaving students with no tangible understanding of college life.

üí∞
Financial Constraints

First-generation families can't afford college visits (transportation, lodging, time off work). Students miss crucial exposure to campus culture, dorms, classrooms, and student resources.

üéØ
Lack of Representation

Students struggle to "see themselves" at elite institutions. Without peer role models or campus exposure, college feels abstract and inaccessible‚Äîleading to lower application rates.

‚è∞
Information Overload

16 UNC campuses with different programs, cultures, and admission requirements. Students and parents feel overwhelmed navigating options without hands-on exploration.

Research & Insights

I collaborated with GEAR UP NC program directors, high school counselors, and target students (ages 14-17) to understand barriers and aspirations through surveys, interviews, and pilot testing.

Key Research Findings

1
Campus Visits = Aspiration Multiplier

Students who physically visit a college campus are 3x more likely to apply and 2.5x more likely to enroll (NCAN study). But 78% of GEAR UP NC students had never visited any university.

2
Mobile-First Generation

92% of target students own smartphones (vs. 45% owning laptops). Mobile is their primary internet access point‚Äîmaking a desktop website insufficient.

3
VR Creates Presence & Emotional Connection

Pilot testing showed 360¬∞ VR videos triggered emotional responses ("I felt like I was really there!") that static photos/videos couldn't replicate‚Äîcritical for building campus belonging.

4
Parents Are Decision-Makers

First-generation parents heavily influence college decisions but feel intimidated by admissions processes. They needed reassurance about campus safety, affordability, and academic support.

My approach centered on "Accessible Immersion"‚Äîleveraging VR's emotional power while keeping technology simple enough for non-tech-savvy teens and parents.

1. Low-Barrier VR Entry

Traditional VR requires expensive headsets ($300-$600). I designed for Google Cardboard‚Äîa $5 foldable viewer that transforms any smartphone into a VR device. Students can watch in 360¬∞ mode OR toggle Cardboard mode for full immersion.

  • ‚úì No app downloads on campus tours‚Äîworks via mobile browser

2. Narrative-Driven Campus Tours

Instead of generic campus footage, I structured content around student storytelling‚Äîcurrent UNC students guiding viewers through their daily routines, favorite spots, and academic programs.

3. Action-Oriented Architecture

Inspiration without action paths leads nowhere. Every campus tour ends with clear next steps: Apply Now, Request Info, Contact Admissions, Financial Aid Resources.

Design Lessons Learned

Accessibility Must Be Baked In, Not Added Later

Early testing revealed students with hearing impairments couldn't engage with video content. Retrofitting captions was costly. Now I design with accessibility from day one.

Technology Can't Replace Human Connection

VR tours inspired students, but conversion to applications required follow-up mentorship. We integrated email opt-ins to connect students with admissions counselors post-exploration.

Mobile Performance = User Retention

Students abandoned the app if videos took >5 seconds to load. Aggressive video compression and CDN optimization were critical for rural areas with 3G connectivity.
